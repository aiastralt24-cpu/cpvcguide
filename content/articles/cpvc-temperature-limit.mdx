## Why this number needs context

A quoted service temperature is useful only when it is tied to the actual system. Readers often search for a single maximum number, but practical design work depends on how long the pipe sees elevated temperatures and what pressure exists at that temperature.

## What service temperature means

Service temperature is the rated operating context a manufacturer or standard uses to describe the upper thermal range for the material. It should be read alongside pressure information and application guidance rather than as a standalone promise.

## Where heat becomes a real decision point

The most practical decision points are near heaters, in hot-water branches, and in systems with repeated thermal cycling. Those are the areas where CPVC selection and installation detail matter most.

## Why derating matters

Higher temperature usually means lower pressure capacity for plastic piping systems. That is why a good technical page does not stop at a top temperature figure. It should also explain pressure derating and route readers to the next decision they need to make.

## A manufacturer reference can be contextual, not promotional

Products such as Astral CPVC Pro are commonly described around a 93 degrees C service-temperature context, but the useful editorial move is to explain what that number means and where it stops being enough on its own.
